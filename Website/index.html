Subsystems we’ve developed
 
 
Mechanical system design resulted in the use of a steel frame with thin, clear plastic sheeting for the game's structure. This is light, strong, dramatically less expensive, and the clear plastic allows for a compelling, exciting game interaction. A wooden base plate provides a place to mount components. Actuators are hidden from view in the base of the device. Power transmission occurs with timing belts up to the point of actuation. Actuation includes "flippers" which hit the ball, "windmills" which rotate and hit the ball, and jets of air which affect the ball's travel. the ball is collected at the end of geme play with a funnel device.

Electrical system design resulted in the use of H-bridges and or transistors to provide thepower and control requirements for actuation. This minamilistic circuitry provided less functionality than a traditional "motor driver board" but significantly reduced complexity and allowed us to easily power and control all our many actuators without using a significant amount of the limited GPIO available.

Software system design used external interupts to start and end the game timer when the ball passed through a break beam sensor. Software scored the game based on the time since start and introduces an unpredictable delay in actuation response which increases as game play extends. This increases the chalenge of the game as the player gains skill.

System design involved integrating the electrical, mechanical, and software systems as well as developing a compelling game interaction for the user. Game design focused on allowing the userto control how they hit the ball with the provided actuation methods. We wanted the actuators to be fun and interesting to interface with. Aditional work went into making the game easier to play and reducing the possibility that the ball would fall too fast for the user to ever hit it. Integrating sub-systems faced its largest issue with the limited amount of GPIO available with the PIC24. Therefore, the software needed to reduce the number of inputs needed to control all the actuators desired by the mechanical system. This was largely solved through the electrical system and by making design trade-offs.
